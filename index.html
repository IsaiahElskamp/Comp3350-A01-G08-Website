01 — Vision Statement

Why GiG Exists

"GiG is a platform for contractors and businesses to easily find jobs, and for clients to quickly connect with professionals who can bid to complete tasks for them."

People often have difficulty finding reliable contractors who offer fair pricing, and contractors often face inconsistent job opportunities and trouble expanding their reach within a community. GiG bridges this gap by providing a dedicated space where work opportunities for professionals and service needs for clients can simultaneously be fulfilled.

GiG will be an improvement over current marketplace applications as it is specifically for finding jobs and task-doers, whereas other applications may only have that as a side feature. This means GiG contains a much deeper and more fleshed-out service for that specific need.

The goal is to create trust, long-term reliability, and accountability between clients and professionals within communities. Since businesses bid against each other, customers always receive the benefit of industry competitiveness.

Professionals will have quick access to local side jobs, visibility into what is most needed in the market, and the ability to make lasting client connections for future jobs.

Design Decision / Business Rules

A professional is allowed to place multiple bids on the same request. This behaviour is intentional to allow:

  • Updating pricing over time
  • Competing strategies (e.g., faster vs. cheaper bids)
  • Flexibility in negotiation-like scenarios

GiG facilitates connections between clients and professionals. The primary goal is to enable request posting, bidding, and selection of professionals.

⚠ Payment processing is intentionally out of scope. Once a client selects a professional, financial transactions are assumed to occur outside the system.

02 — Users

Intended Users

User Groups
Group 01

Clients

Anyone who needs something fixed, a task done, or general help with something they cannot complete themselves. Clients benefit from the flexibility of the bid system — competing professionals drive prices down and quality up.

Group 02

Professionals

People looking for side jobs, general contractors, or those involved in businesses. GiG gives professionals quick access to local jobs, visibility into market demand, and the ability to build lasting client relationships.

03 — Features

Major Features

General

Role Based Accounts

Users can either sign up as a Client or Professional. Each account type has different attributes, and accesses a different side of the interface for the app.

Client

Request Management

Clients can create a request for a job they want completed. They can also view all of their current and past requests, as well as delete any active requests.

Clients can accept a bid placed on a request, then complete the request once the job has been done.

General

Reviews

Clients can place reviews on pros once they complete a request. Clients can view reviews for any pro that has placed a bid on their request, and pros can view their own reviews.

Professional

Bid Management

Pros can view all active requests made by clients, and place bids on them. They can also view all bids they have made on active requests.

When placing a bid, pros can enter the cost they are willing to do the job for, and the time that works best for their schedule.

Professional

Appointments

After a pro's bid is accepted by a client, an appointment is created which contains details about the job. These appointments can be viewed by the pro, and they can see all current and past appointments.

General

Account Editing

Both professionals and clients can edit their profile information at any time.

07 — Development Postmortem

Reflecting on the Process

Iteration 2 Retrospective: Improvement Area

After our Iteration 2 retrospective, we identified that testing should be our primary focus after the completion of every feature. We realized that since iteration 1, we developed strong unit tests, but but did not carry out enough manual end-to-end testing, which led to minor bugs and usability issues being skipped over when they could have been fixed. Most of our tests were based on quick scenarios and did not involve edge-case testing. We also realized in our early iterations that we had little focus on the user experience of the application.

What changed: To counter our lack of end user testing, we implemented a simple QA test cycle every time a major feature was pushed. Each member of the group tried the changes themselves, and attempted to break them through rigorous edge cases and non-happy path tests. Once each member gave their approval we moved on to the next feature. Then, at the end of iteration we all got together as a group and tested the app as a whole in order to find user experience issues that we could improve on.

Outcome: The changes we made in our approach resulted in noticeable improvements in both code quality and user experience. By the time we conducted final integration and demonstration, we ran into far fewer bugs, and this also reduced the need for last-minute fixes, which weren't as concrete in our last few iterations. This led the team to spend more time refining the code rather than fixing brutal errors and inconsistencies in the application a day before submission. This iteration also made us genuinely appreciate the value of an early feedback strategy, and honestly, it's one of the biggest wins of following an Agile approach. Peer testing proved particularly valuable in addressing usability issues early. This resulted in smoother navigation and better user experience.

✦ What Went Well

  • Strong team coordination kept collaboration smooth and progressive
  • Tasks were assigned based on individual strengths, boosting efficiency and quality
  • Clear user story estimation helped set realistic, achievable goals each iteration
  • Deadlines were met consistently, reflecting the team's commitment and reliability

✦ What We'd Change

  • Enforce a stricter Git branching strategy to maintain code stability
  • Improve accuracy of time estimation to avoid underestimation or overestimation of feature completion timelines
  • Establish a structured testing and feature finalization workflow
  • Better define feature scope early so that all team members remain on the same page

✦ Architecture & Design Decisions

  • Built on a five-layer architecture where each layer has a single responsibility and only communicates with its immediate neighbours
  • Data flows unidirectionally that is from user input through Business logic and Models, down to Persistence, then back up to update the UI
  • There was no structured way for clients and professionals to access each other's contact information, highlighting a gap in the system design which we fixed in the final stage
  • Our app intentionally allows pro to place multiple bids on the same request to allow flexibility.

✦ Lessons Learned

  • Catching issues early saves a lot of trouble down the line and leads to better design decisions
  • Peer feedback is underrated, outside perspectives catch what you miss and make the product noticeably better
  • Starting early and maintaining steady progress improves quality and reduces last minute hustle
  • Functionality isn't enough, if the application is not usable, it's not done, UX matters just as much as the code
